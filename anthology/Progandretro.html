Progress and Retrogression

By Julio Cortazar 

Progress and Retrogression

They invented a kind of glass which let flies through. The fly would come, push a little with his head and pop, he was on the other side. Enormous happiness on the part of the fly.

All this was ruined by a Hungarian scientist when he discovered that the fly could enter but not get out, or vice versa, because he didn't know what gimmick was involved in the glass or the flexibility of its fibers, for was very fibroid. They immediately invented a fly trap with a sugar cube inside, and many flies perished miserably. So ended any possible brotherhood with these animals, who are deserving of better luck.

 

The Purpose of Technology in Progress and Retrogression

Julio Cortázar, in their poem Progress and Retrogression, uses personification and changing tone to show how technology can be subverted for more malicious uses. 

The poem personifies the fly to give it human emotions. This allows us to empathize with the fly and see why the use of the glass to trap them is, in the opinion of the poet, very wrong. The fly is shown to have "enormous happiness" when it makes it through to the other side of the glass. There is talk of “brotherhood with these animals”. Though we cannot, in actuality, communicate effectively with flies, the idea of it gives the poem weight and carries emotion. 

The poem also creates changes in tone, going from enthusiastic to lamenting. This shift is supported by its structure and diction. Free verse and stanza breaks are used to show contrast before and after the invention was turned into a trap. In the first paragraph there is a much lighter tone, using language like “enormous happiness” but in the second paragraph, the mood is darker and phrases such as “perished miserably” are used. The subject also shifts from a wider “they” scope, to a directed “he” one. Blame is put specifically onto the scientist instead of general praise for humanity, as in the first paragraph. This is showing how one person can ruin something positive that many others had worked hard on. 

Julio Cortázar presents how positive inventions can be made into weapons and used to harm others. They show this through their implementation of personification and tone shifts.
